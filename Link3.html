Green Hydrogen

Hydrogen has always been an intriguing possible replacement for fossil fuels.
It burns cleanly, emitting no carbon dioxide; it’s energy dense,
so it’s a good way to store power from on-and-off renewable sources;
and you can make liquid synthetic fuels that are drop-in replacements for gasoline or diesel. But most hydrogen up to now has been made from natural gas;
the process is dirty and energy intensive. The rapidly dropping cost of solar and wind power means green hydrogen is now cheap enough to be practical.
Simply zap water with electricity, and presto, you’ve got hydrogen. Europe is leading the way, beginning to build the needed infrastructure.
Peter Fairley argues that such projects are just a first step to an envisioned global network of electrolysis plants that run on solar and wind power,
churning out clean hydrogen
